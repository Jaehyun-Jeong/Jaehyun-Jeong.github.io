Can we simply apply existing off-policy methods to leverage offline data when learning online, without offline RL pre-training or explicit imitation terms that privilege the prior offline data? The primary objecive of the authors is to answer this question. However, to do this, authors had to solve three main problems.

* Very expensive export data from real robots
* Sparsity of reward signal in robotics
* Sample efficiency in offline data

There have been methods to address these problems with pre-training and imitation-term. Yet, they are not sample-efficient and require reliable data source and it is very expensive. On top of that, these algorithms are very sensitive to OOD(out-of-distribution) due to their nature of learning.

In terms of these problems, RLPD provides robustness in dataset and sample-efficiency. More precisely, RLPD can train with suboptimal data and expert data and even with off-policy data. authors proposed three distinct methods to mitigate these problems based on SAC(Soft Actor-Critic). These are called the **"Symmetric Sampling"**, **"Layer Normalization"**, and **"Random Ensemble Distilation"**.

## Symmetric Sampling

![](/assets/images/2026-04-02-rlpd/symmetric-sampling.png)

Overall idea of symmetric sampling is extremely simple. It builds batch from 50% of replay buffer and 50% of offline data buffer. In spite of this simplicity, it resolves OOD problem with stability, alleviating the restriction of data with sub-optimal trajectories.

* **Offline data**: Expert Demonstration (small) + Sub-optimal trajectories (large) collected by sub-optimal policies.

## Layer Normalization

![](/assets/images/2026-04-02-rlpd/layer-normalization.png)

Batch normalization normalize each feature values through samples. Unlike batch normalization, layer normalization normalize each sample values through layer outputs. To provide further clarification, layer normalization calculates mean and standard deviation from layer's outputs after activation in a sample not a batch.

Through this method, RLPD can mitigate Q-value overestimation problem in OOD observations. Because the layer normalization constrain Q-value into weight's norm like the below.

$$
Q^*(s, a) = \sum_{s', r} p(s', r | s, a) \left[ r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q^*(s', a') \right]
$$<br>
*Bellman optimal equation triggers Q-value overestimation*

$$
\begin{aligned}
\|Q_{\theta,w}(s, a)\| &= \|w^T \mathrm{relu}(\psi_\theta(s, a))\| \\
&\le \|w\| \|\mathrm{relu}(\psi_\theta(s, a))\| \le \|w\| \|\psi(s, a)\| \\
&\le \|w\| (\because Layer Norm)
\end{aligned}
$$<br>
*Layer normalization constrain Q-values within weights' norm*

## Random Ensemble Distilation + High UTD(update-to-data) ratio

UTD means the number of updates per batch. As a result of high UTD, the algorithm can use data more efficiently, and it means sample efficient. Ironically, other research have shown that it can lead statistical over-fitting (Li et al., 2022) due to the multiple updates with the same samples. To ameliorate this, authors have suggested to use random ensemble distilation.

Random ensemble distilation tackle the callenge of over-fitting like the method of DDQN and TD3, which means that they are using multiple value functions. In the context of random ensemble distilation, it utilizes an ensemble of $E$ Q-models and select 2 random Q-models and update all of Q-models. And, when it updates the policy, it leverages $E$ different Q-models to estimate true Q-value by averging them.
